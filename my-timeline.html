My Timeline: Impact of Historical Events on Canadian Law & Society

  • 1066

  • Norman conquest of England

    Queen Mathilda's Tapestry [1]

  • This invasion was an attempt to take over the throne which was under King Edward and his successor Harold Godwinson. William I, the Duke of Normandy and soon-to-be Conqueror, challenged candidates Harald III of Norway and Godwinson's brother Tostig by invading south England. Through a series of battles, deadly military tactics, and threats from rebels, William I claimed victory [2].

    After the victory, William I established the ideas that would form feudalism, a prevailing form of government based on full ownership of land by a Lord (one of higher power). William assigned people as landowners, and landowners had a mutual obligation to provide military services. This system of government made legislation and social justice directly tied to a monarch, who was in charge of all lawmaking, implementation and interpretation of law.

  • 1066-1087

  • William I

    William the Conqueror, first Norman King of England [25]

  • William I and great grandson Henry II used feudal system of government.

  • 1153-1189

  • Henry II

  • Disagreements were settled by the king, it became overwhelming, circuit courts were developed. Judges tried to use previous decision to inform current cases.

    This is the seed of common law [25].

  • 1763

  • The Royal Proclamation Treaty

  • The basis for the relationship between Federal, Provincial and aboriginal groups. One of the first legal documents that recognize the Indigenous peoples and their title to land rights.

    The formation of the treaty occurred after the end of the Seven Years War (1756 - 1763), where Great Britain claimed victory over France, and was now ruling over land that would form both Canada and the United States. It allowed legislative authority to be held under three parties: the governor, the governor's council, and the assembly. Although this came before the establishment of Canada as a country, the treaty was responsible for the institutional relationship between the government and aboriginal groups that is still in effect today.

  • 1774

  • The Quebec Act

    Province of Quebec in 1774 [4]

  • Enacted by the British Parliament, this served to preserve the French Canadian population and revoke the assimilation attempts caused by the Royal Proclamation Treaty [3]. This event is seen as an act of gaining the trust of French-spoken people and establishing rights to property and religious freedom in Quebec. However, this was to the dismay of the American colonies, serving an important catalyst to the American Revolutionary War [3]. This event can be seen as one of the earliest forms of English-French peace relations in Canada, and would influence many legislative decisions made in Quebec to preserve human rights and culture.

  • 1775-1783

  • The American Revolutionary War

    Surrender of Lord Cornwallis, a British army general [6]

  • A significant event of the American Revolution (1765 - 1791), this war contributed to the political independence of North American colonies that were held under Great Britain. The effects of the war were of great magnitude, resulting in establishment of the American Declaration of Independence, and a large number of unfavorable settlers emigrating towards Canadian territory [5]. Those who emigrated were seen as British loyalists, and thus developed a culture shaped around conservatism. This can be seen as the birth of Canadian culture, and an example of influence from Britain's parliamentary government that would form the nation to come.

  • 1791

  • The Constitutional Act of 1791

    Division of Upper and Lower Canada [10]

  • Synonymously known as the Canada Act of 1791, this was enacted by the Parliament of Britain to segregate the province of Quebec into two regions: Upper and Lower Canada. Key justifications of this act included access to equal rights, woman's right to vote, and establishment of provincial legislation [10]. Upper Canada mainly used Common law, whereas Lower Canada used Old french law [10]. This act, along with the Quebec Act, showed the disparities in lawmaking between the English and French regions. An example of the role of English-French conflicts in Canadian law.

  • 1837-1838

  • Rebellion Acts

    Destruction of steamboats on the Niagara Falls, 1837 [11]

  • A growing frustration with the monarchial government due to its poor handling of legislation and lack of reform led to a series of violent, rebellious acts in both Upper and Lower Canada. The rebellion from the public lead to significant changes such as the reformation of Upper and Lower Canada into a unified state with a sole responsible government [11]. This came into effect with the Act of Union in 1840, and eventually the British North American Act of 1867. Thus, it is no secret that this event was crucial to the development of Canada as a confederation.

  • 1840

  • Act of Union

    Baron Sydenham, governor general of the province of Canada [12]

  • Enacted by the British Parliament, it united the colonies of Upper Canada and Lower Canada under one government [12]. Recommended by Lord Durham in his famous Durham Report (1839) after observing the Rebellion acts, a new parliament system was formed, which included elected seats for both regions, and banishment of French institutions on civil law and French as an official language. These anti-French measures drew opposition from Lower Canada, and eventually were repealed. Because of the new responsible government, the public was given the right to negotiate provisions of the act rather than the Crown [12]. This can be interpreted as the earliest example of Canadian democracy.

  • 1861-1865

  • The American Civil War

    Battle of Gettysburg [13]

  • This war was the result of the past four decades of modernization in the American colonies and conflicting values over exploitation through slavery [13]. The resulting expansion of west coast land due to the north's victory feared the people in Canada, prompting the government to commence the confederation of Canada. This was a crucial move to avoid a potential invasion that would have threatened the people and decades of progress towards a well-established legislature.

  • 1867

  • The British North America Act

    Canada's Fathers of Confederation [14]

  • Arguably the most significant event in Canadian history and law. This was the culmination of the merger of the former two regions, the American Civil War, and centuries of development in lawmaking and governance. This act solidified Canada as a true country, with a central governing body. Provinces had more freedom with their own government and French became a recognized language. It established the basis of the Constitution, which would be further expanded upon in the next century.

  • 1867-1928

  • Re-adjustments to Canadian culture

    Parliament Buildings, circa 1900 [15]

  • Following confederation, the notions that were set by the framers of the Constitution would take six decades to be fully realized after the acts of 1867. In particular, the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council (JCPC), a British court of appeal, was not in favor of the key ideas of a true central government and conservative politics. They were rooted in liberalism and many of its practices were interpreted as favoring provincial governments. This can be viewed as the result of British culture interfering with Canadian culture, and there needed to be significant changes to the legislation that would allow Canada to stay true to its founding fathers.

  • 1929

  • Edwards v. Canada (AG)

    PM King and the Valiant Five, a women's rights advocate group [16]

  • A landmark case for women's rights. This is an example of the JCPC utilizing their power over legislation for the advancement of gender equality in the Senate. The main impact of this case was the dominance of a liberal judiciary approach to the Constitution, where present day society influences the law rather than old legal documents or events from the past.

  • 1931-1933

  • The Statute of Westminster

    PM King and O.D. Skelton, key figures in the agreement between Britain and Canada [17]

  • A huge turning point in Canadian Law, and a milestone in Canadian independence from Britain. Enacted by the British Parliament, it bestowed legislative sovereignty onto the government of Canada. As a result, the Supreme Court would no longer require an appeal from the JCPC to enact any federal or provincial law. This also allowed Canada to minimize the influence of British politics into Canadian culture. An act that finally allowed Canada to restore the notions set in the Constitution Act of 1867.

  • 1934-1939

  • The Great Depression

    A "dust bowl" in the prairies, caused by drought [18]

  • This global recession period played a huge role in how Canada's federal government came to dominate all country affairs, both federal and provincial. To address the rising unemployment rates from the great depression, the government established a welfare state that provided insurance, pension, agriculture expansion, and social assistance. This helped the provinces that were either experiencing job shortages or drought conditions. After all, the timing of the Statue of Westminster did not help Canada's recovery from the depression. The now sovereign federal government had many legislative responsibilities to fulfill because of this.

  • 1940-1946

  • World War II

    Allied Conference in Quebec, 1939 [19]

  • Another global event that affected legislation and human rights in Canada. Because of persecutions of Jewish people and minority groups throughout the war, social movements became popular with its campaigns for cultural and equal rights. Members of the Canadian Parliament would go on to contribute to the United Nations Charter and the Universal Declaration of Human Rights, both of which are considered milestones in human rights history. This was the second time that the federal government enacted the War Measures Act, giving them power to enact any bill or law for the purpose of national security. However, the deportation acts of Japanese people in Canada were widely controversial but luckily never came into fruition after the war ended.

  • 1960

  • The Canadian Bill of Rights

    PM Diefenbaker (left), who enacted the bill in 1960 [20]

  • Following World War II, the popularity of social movements led to the recognition of human rights in the federal government as part of the new Canadian Bill of Rights. This document established the right of an individual under federal laws and the basic rights to life, liberty, freedom of expression and values. Although an important step in the protection of human rights in law, the scope of the bill was limited to federal legislation, leaving provinces with their own decisions on jurisdiction practices that may or may not adhere to the bill’s guidelines. This presented issues that would become clear in the following years.

  • 1960-1970

  • Liberalization of Canadian Society

    An Indigenous group rally [21]

  • Along with the introduction of the Bill of Rights, this decade saw the liberal movements at the forefront of the public. Because of this, jurisdictions faced difficulty with implementing laws because they failed to consider the Constitution as a living tree doctrine (i.e. adapt to the present day circumstances). Another challenge was addressing laws that conflicted with the Bill of Rights. In the R. v. Drybones (1970) case, a section of the Indian Act was repealed due to its violation of Indigenous rights. And in Quebec, the presence of separatist organizations such as the FLQ pressured the government to enact the War Measures Act for the third time. The government responses to both events highlighted this disconnect between federal and provincial laws, prompting politicians to find a solution to protecting civil rights in all forms of government.

  • 1973

  • Recognition of the Aboriginal title

    Frank Calder, a key member of the Nisga'a group [22]

  • A milestone for Indigenous land rights in Canadian Law came during the Calder et al. v. Attorney General of British Columbia trial. The overwhelming majority ruled that the Indeigenous rights to land had its place in the law, and that the occupation of land by Aboriginal people needed to be recognized when land disputes were carried out by opposing parties. These notions would be further expanded upon with the Delgamuukw v. BC (1997) case that allowed Indigenous groups to negotiate and prove their title to an area of land.

  • 1982

  • The Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms

    PM Pierre Trudeau and Queen Elizabeth II solidifying the Charter [23]

  • Perhaps the most important and significant change to the Constitution since the Bill of Rights. This was the result of Prime Minister Pierre Trudeau’s efforts to integrate rights and freedoms into the Constitution of Canada, along with the growing trend of liberalism from the past two decades. The charter would affect all aspects of government including the Monarchy, and lead to greater protection of an individual under the law. It incorporated everything from Indigenous rights to freedom of expression and most notably, the Notwithstanding clause. This clause allowed the government to override sections of the Charter when implementing laws. In retrospect, one may view this event as the result of democratization of Canadian society over the past 100 years.

  • 1983-2022

  • Free Trade, Civil Disobedience, and changes to the Law

    Freedom Convoy in Ottawa, 2022 [24]

  • Since the inception of the Charter, there have been changes to previous laws such as the War Measures Act into a more reformed Emergencies Act in 1988. International law was incorporated through fair trade agreements such as the North American Free Trade Agreement (NAFTA) in 1994. And there have been cases of civil disobedience throughout the country, most recently the Freedom Convoy protest in 2022, where the Emergencies Act was invoked for the first time. Notable changes during this period include provisions on hate speech and child pornography, which were seen as controversial for normalizing taboo subjects in society. One could view these changes as an effect of guaranteeing human rights to a large and diverse population. This begs the question of whether there will need to be a Constitutional amendment in the future.
